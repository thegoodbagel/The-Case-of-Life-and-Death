Shradha and Abrianna

Something groundbreaking happened on June 24, 2022. Something that would forever change the fate of women in America.

52 years ago, under the anonymous guise "Jane Roe", a pregnant woman filed a lawsuit for legalizing abortion in Texas. After three years at court, the US Supreme court reached a landmark decision. In 1973, abortion became legalized nationwide. This historic case was known as Roe v. Wade . On Friday, the Supreme court overturned Roe v. Wade. Individual states are now permitted to re-ban abortion—and they will.

Past

How has abortion played out in history?

The earliest documented abortions took place in Egypt at aroun 1550 BCE. In ancient Greece and Rome, abortion was an extensive practice that likely went unpunished. Throughout the most of Western history, abortion was not criminalized as long as it carried out before a stage called "quickening", when women could feel the first movements of the fetus, roughly 3-5 months.

A shift came about in the 19th century, when public opinion began to turn against abortion. The primary factors were that physics were arguing that conception began before quickening and that abortion providers were often untrained, unprofessional doctors. Thus started the emergence of anti-abortion laws.

In 1821, the first anti-abortion law took place in Connecticut, which punished the providers of abortion-inducing substances. In 1845, New York passed a law that punished abortion receivers with a prison sentence. By the 1910, every state in the US had some form of abortion restriction policies.

However, in the 1960s, abortion laws took a turn again, this time towards liberalization. A number of pro-abortion organizations were formed, including the Society for Humane Abortion and the NARAL Pro-Choice America.

Present

What was Roe v. Wade and what did the overturn do—or undo?

In 1973, the landmark judgement of the Supreme Court in Roe v. Wade legalized abortion across the nation. In 1992, the Planned Parenthood v. Casey case reaffirmed Roe v. Wade while loosening the framework for state policies on abortion.

Following the 2010 midterm elections, state restrictions on abortion took a sharp turn due to an increase of the Republican party. The types of bans included ultrasound laws, criminalization of abortions based on the sex or race of a fetus, fetal abnormality laws, "fetal heartbeat" laws, trigger laws that would go into place if Roe v. Wade was overturned, and much more.

On Jun 24, 2022, the Supreme Court overturned Roe v. Wade, asserting that "the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion; Roe and Casey are overruled; and the authority to regulate abortion is returned to the people and their elected representatives".

The first draft of the majority opinion was leaked in early May with Justice Alito's vehement condemnation of abortion:

"Roe was egregiously wrong from the start. Its reasoning was exceptionally weak, and the decision has had damaging consequences."

Future

What does America's future hold for us?

Without constitutional coverance, states are now free to apply their own regulations on abortions.

According to the Guttmacher Institute, "26 states are certain or likely to ban abortion". Here is an interactive map that shows how restrictive abortion policies are by state, along with demographics and abortion statistics.

Additionally, New York Times explains that "in many cases, courts still must determine the fate of these restrictions. Lawmakers in other states are expected to propose new laws either to restrict or to protect abortion access."
